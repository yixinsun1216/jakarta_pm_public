\subsubsection*{Envelope Tightening: Back-of-the-envelope.}

Following \citep{chen_review_2011}, in steady-state, indoor PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations $C_{in}$ can be expressed as a function of outdoor PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations $C_{out}$, the air exchange rate due to infiltration \textit{a}, the penetration factor \textit{P}, the particle deposition rate \textit{k} and the indoor-source strength by volume \textit{S}:
\begin{align*}
    C_{in} &= \frac{a P C_{out} +S}{a+k}.
\end{align*}
The coefficient $\beta$ estimated in Eq. (1)  can be shown to correspond to the infiltration factor $F_{inf} = \frac{a P}{a + k}$. For the central back-of-the-envelope calculation, we set $P = 1$ and $k = 0.5 h^{-1}$ and solve for the implied air exchange rate
\begin{align}
    a &= \frac{\hat{F}_{inf} k}{P - \hat{F}_{inf}}
\end{align}
Given $(a, P, k)$, we recover indoor-source strength by volume as the residual
\begin{align*}
    S &= (a + k) C_{in} - a P C_{out}.
\end{align*}
Robust range: we vary the deposition loss rate $k$ over $[0.2, 1.5]~(h^{-1})$ and the penetration factor $P$ over $[0.8, 1.0]$. We evaluate the four corner combinations $(k_{min}, P_{min}), (k_{min}, P_{max}), (k_{max}, P_{min}), (k_{max}, P_{max})$; for each we solve for the implied $a=\frac{\hat F_{inf}k}{P-\hat F_{inf}}$ and recompute $S=(C_{in}-\hat F_{inf}C_{out})(a+k)$.
\\

This model can be leveraged in a simple back-of-the-envelope calculation to assess the impact of interventions that reduce air exchange (targeting \textit{a}) or reduce particle penetration of incoming air (targeting \textit{P}) on indoor PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations. Table \ref{SI_BoE_table} lists the data inputs and external parameters used as well as the main results, split by income and smoking status.
\\

The back-of-the-envelope exercise suggests that reducing particle penetration (lowering $P$) mechanically decreases indoor PM$_{2.5}$ in all households—under the convention that $P$ applies only to outdoor-to-indoor particle entry—whereas weatherization (reducing $a$) can have ambiguous effects because it also limits dilution of indoor emissions. In our central calibration, weatherization increases indoor PM$_{2.5}$ in low-income households, and this conclusion is robust over the parameter range considered. In contrast, weatherization decreases concentrations in high-income non-smoking households throughout the robust range; for high-income smoking households, the effect remains small and can change sign over the robust range. Precisely quantifying the impacts of interventions that alter $a$ and $P$ would require stronger empirical identification of these parameters, which is beyond the scope of this paper.

\subsubsection*{LMG $R^2$ Decomposition.} 
To complement the mean contribution decomposition in the main text, we decompose the $R^2$ of Equation 1 across predictors using the Lindeman-Merenda-Gold (LMG) method \citep{lindeman_introduction_1980}. 
In multiple regressions, the contribution of a predictor to explained variance depends on which other predictors are already in the model, rendering simple sequential decompositions sensitive to arbitrary ordering choices. LMG resolves the attribution problem that arises under predictor correlation by averaging each predictor's marginal $R^2$ contribution over all possible orderings in which predictors could enter the model. This method thus provides a consistent decomposition of explained variance. We implement this using the \texttt{relaimpo} package in \texttt{R} \citep{gromping_relative_2006}. 
The ranking of sources by $R^2$ contribution is consistent with the mean contribution decomposition in the main text: outdoor ambient pollution accounts for the largest share, followed by household smoking and waste burning, with kitchen source, cooking hour, and distance to main road contributing minimally. For pollution spikes, smoking is by far the dominant contributor, while waste burning also contributes substantially.
